The Last Lighthouse

The storm had been raging for three days when Elena finally spotted the light.

She had been adrift since the wave took her boat, clinging to a broken plank and
counting the seconds between lightning strikes. Her arms ached. Her lips were cracked
from salt. She had stopped believing in luck somewhere around hour forty.

But there it was — a steady pulse through the darkness. One flash, then nothing,
then one flash again.

She swam toward it.

The lighthouse keeper was an old man named Thomas who smelled of pipe smoke and
strong tea. He said very little as he pulled her from the rocks and wrapped her in
a wool blanket. He set a kettle on the stove and disappeared into another room.

"How long has this lighthouse been here?" Elena asked when he returned.

"Long enough," he said, pouring the tea.

She looked around at the bare stone walls, the worn wooden floors, the single cot
in the corner. "Do you ever get lonely?"

Thomas set his cup down and gazed out through the salt-blurred glass at the churning
sea below.

"I keep the light," he said simply. "Someone always needs it."

When the storm broke the next morning, a rescue boat found Elena standing at the
top of the lighthouse, watching the horizon clear. She turned to wave goodbye to
Thomas, but the lantern room was empty.

The logbook on the table was open to a page dated forty years ago. The last entry
read: *Light maintained. All is well.*

Elena walked down to the boat in silence. She never told anyone what she had seen.
But for the rest of her life, whenever she passed a lighthouse at night, she always
waved.
